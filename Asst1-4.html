Based on these data displays and logistic regression results, sex and socioeconomic status (defined here by poverty and education levels) do impact the contribution of smoking on the risk of disease. However, education did not significantly modify the effects of smoking on disease rate. As seen in the logistic regression, none of the interaction terms for smoking and education status were significant (Pr(>|z|) of less than 0.05).

Holding everything else constant, the difference in log odds of disease for those who are poor is 0.6036 higher than those who are not poor. Thus the odds ratio for the poor is 1.8288 times that of those who are not poor of similar education and sex, which shows the additional affect of being poor on smoking. This can be seen in the data display as the ratios of lung cancer in smokers to non-smokers amongst the poor group was greater than that of the not-poor group.

Holding everything else constant, the difference in log odds of disease for females is 0.6405 lower than the log odds of disease for males. Thus the odds ratio for females is 0.527 times that of males of similar education and poverty status, which shows the additional affect of being female on smoking. This can be seen in the graphical display as the percentage of lung cancer in male smokers was over twice the percentage of lung cancer in male non-smokers. The percentages of lung cancer in females were relatively comparable between smokers and non-smokers.
